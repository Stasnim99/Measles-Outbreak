About measles

What the disease is, how it spreads, and why it takes hold so quickly in under-vaccinated communities.

Symptoms

  • High fever, cough, runny nose, and red, watery eyes 7–14 days after exposure
  • A red, blotchy rash that usually starts on the face and spreads down the body
  • Tiny white spots inside the mouth (Koplik spots) in some cases
  • Contagious from about 4 days before to 4 days after the rash appears

How contagious is it

  • Measles is among the most contagious diseases known. It spreads through the air and can remain in a room for up to two hours after an infected person has left
  • If one person has measles, up to 9 out of 10 unvaccinated people nearby will typically become infected
  • A community generally needs about 95% MMR vaccination coverage to keep outbreaks from taking hold

Possible complications

  • Common: ear infections, diarrhea
  • Serious: pneumonia, encephalitis (brain swelling)
  • Can be fatal, particularly in young children and immunocompromised people
  • Children under 5 make up about 21% of 2026 cases

2026 in context

  • 2026 is on pace to be one of the worst years for measles in the United States since elimination was declared in 2000
  • No deaths have been confirmed in 2026 as of this writing; three were reported in 2025
  • If transmission continues uninterrupted for 12 months, the U.S. risks losing its official measles elimination status, a determination expected in November 2026

Prevention & vaccination

The MMR (measles, mumps, rubella) vaccine remains the single most effective tool against measles.

MMR vaccine effectiveness

  • 2 doses: about 97% effective at preventing measles
  • 1 dose: about 93% effective
  • Protection against measles is generally lifelong after the full 2-dose series
  • Standard schedule: first dose at 12–15 months, second at 4–6 years

Why vaccination status matters in this outbreak

  • About 92% of 2026 U.S. cases are in people who are unvaccinated or whose vaccination status is unknown
  • Outbreaks are concentrated in under-vaccinated communities, which explains why cases cluster tightly in specific counties and states rather than spreading evenly across the country
  • Reviewing your own and your family's MMR records, and catching up on any missed doses, remains the most direct way to reduce personal risk
If you suspect that you or a family member has measles, call your doctor or clinic before visiting in person. This allows staff to take precautions and avoid exposing other patients in the waiting room.
